Introduction

Our project focuses on creating insightful visualizations based on the distribution and characteristics of stars in the Milky Way. By mapping stars according to their brightness, temperature, and spatial position, we uncover how stellar populations vary across the sky and better understand the structure of our galactic environment.

Modern astronomical data has allowed us to grow our understanding of space and the night sky by a tremendous amount. Research regarding the night sky was very limited for a huge portion of human existence until recently. The vast amount of complex astronomical data gives us flexibility with visualizations and insights, revealing patterns that were previously hidden to humanity for millennia.

Data Description

Source: The dataset is derived from the HEASARC Bright Star Catalog (Yale Bright Star Catalogue, 5th Edition), accessed through NASA's High Energy Astrophysics Science Archive Research Center at data.nasa.gov .

Size & Scope: After cleaning and preprocessing, our dataset contains 3,100 bright stars with 64 features. The original catalog includes over 9,000 stars, but we filtered to stars with valid parallax measurements (allowing distance calculations) and complete positional data.

Data Processing: We performed extensive cleaning including converting sexagesimal coordinates to decimal degrees, filtering stars with parallax > 0, computing 3D Cartesian coordinates, creating brightness categories, and calculating distances in parsecs and light-years.

Data Visualizations

Visualization 1 – Star Distribution by Spectral Type (Altair)

This bar chart shows the distribution of stars across different spectral types, revealing that most stars in the dataset belong to the cooler F, G, and K classes, while very hot O and B type stars are comparatively rare. This pattern reflects the natural abundance of stellar types in the observable universe, where massive, hot stars are short-lived and therefore less common. The bar chart design is effective because it allows for direct comparison between discrete spectral categories, making differences in frequency immediately clear. The use of color reinforces the categorical nature of the data, and the interactive legend allows users to isolate and highlight specific spectral types, improving focus and interpretability without cluttering the visualization.

Visualization 2 – Color-Magnitude Relationship

This scatter plot shows the relationship between a star’s color index (B-V), which serves as a proxy for temperature, and its apparent brightness (V magnitude). The visualization reveals that cooler, redder stars (higher B-V values) tend to appear dimmer, while hotter, bluer stars (lower B-V values) are generally brighter, reflecting a fundamental pattern in stellar astronomy. The scatter plot design is effective because it clearly displays this continuous relationship between two quantitative variables, while the use of transparency helps reduce overplotting in dense regions. Interactive tooltips enhance the design by allowing users to explore individual stars in more detail without cluttering the chart.

Visualization 3 - Temperature & Brightness Distributions

These density plots reveal the distribution patterns of stellar characteristics in our dataset, providing a complementary perspective to individual data points. The temperature distribution (top panel) shows that most stars cluster around B-V values of 0.5-1.0, corresponding to yellow-orange stars similar to our Sun in the G and K spectral classes. The smooth curve reveals a roughly normal distribution centered near the mean B-V of approximately 0.65, indicating that intermediate-temperature stars dominate our sample. The brightness distribution (bottom panel) demonstrates that most visible stars have magnitudes between 2 and 5, with a peak around magnitude 3-4. The distribution shows fewer very bright stars (magnitude < 1) and a gradual tail toward dimmer stars (magnitude > 6). This pattern reflects both physical stellar demographics—intrinsically luminous stars are rarer—and observational selection effects, as very faint stars are more difficult to catalog comprehensively. Unlike scatter plots that show relationships between variables, these density plots emphasize frequency and concentration, making it immediately clear which stellar types are most common. The mean lines (dashed) provide reference points for typical values, helping contextualize individual stars seen in other visualizations. Together, these distributions complement the scatter-based visualizations by answering "how many?" rather than "how related?", revealing the underlying population structure of bright stars in our galactic neighborhood.

Visualization 4 - Interactive Celestial Sky Map

This celestial sky map provides a geographic representation of stellar positions across the night sky, using the astronomical coordinate system of Right Ascension (RA) and Declination (Dec)—the celestial equivalents of longitude and latitude. The x-axis spans 0° to 360° of Right Ascension, while the y-axis covers -90° to +90° of Declination, from the south celestial pole to the north celestial pole. The dashed line at Declination 0° marks the celestial equator, dividing the northern and southern celestial hemispheres. The visualization reveals non-uniform distribution patterns across the sky: certain regions show notably higher stellar density while others are more sparse, potentially reflecting the structure of the Milky Way galaxy itself. This map employs multi-channel visual encoding to convey three distinct stellar attributes simultaneously: position (x,y coordinates) maps each star's actual location in the sky, point size encodes apparent brightness with larger circles representing brighter stars, and color represents stellar temperature derived from the B-V color index, with a gradient from blue (hot stars exceeding 10,000K) through white (intermediate temperatures) to orange-red (cool stars below 4,000K). Interactive hover effects dramatically enhance exploration: when a user hovers over any star, it enlarges to twice its normal size, brightens to full opacity, and gains a prominent golden stroke, while a rich tooltip displays the star's name, exact celestial coordinates, magnitude and brightness classification, spectral type, B-V color index, and distance in light-years. This celestial map serves both as a scientific tool for analyzing stellar distributions and as an engaging educational visualization connecting abstract data to the tangible experience of the night sky.

Visualization 5 - Stellar Density Across the Sky

This contour density map reveals concentration patterns in stellar distribution across the celestial sphere using a fundamentally different analytical approach than individual point plotting. While Visualization 4 displays each star as a separate point with interactive filtering, this visualization employs density estimation and contour mapping to show WHERE stars concentrate most heavily across Right Ascension and Declination coordinates. The contour technique aggregates nearby stars into smooth density regions, similar to topographic elevation maps, where each contour line represents areas of similar stellar concentration. The color scale from pale yellow (low density) through orange to deep red (high density) creates an intuitive heat map visualization where "hot" regions contain more stars. Multiple distinct high-density regions emerge as red zones, particularly visible around RA 60-90° and RA 270-300°, with additional concentration near RA 180°. These dense regions likely correspond to our view through the Milky Way's galactic plane and spiral arm structures, where stellar populations naturally concentrate. Lower-density regions (pale yellow) appear at various sky positions, potentially indicating areas where we're looking perpendicular to the galactic disk or through less populated galactic regions. The celestial equator (blue dashed line at 0° declination) provides spatial reference, and the density patterns show asymmetry across this line—suggesting the galactic plane is tilted relative to Earth's equatorial coordinate system. Unlike scatter plots that preserve individual star information (Vis2, Vis4) or frequency histograms showing attribute distributions (Vis3), this density-based aggregation emphasizes spatial concentration patterns—answering "which sky regions are most densely populated?" The smooth contours reduce visual noise from thousands of individual points, making large-scale structural patterns immediately apparent without requiring interaction. This non-interactive design ensures all viewers immediately perceive the density structure, whereas Vis4's filtering requires active exploration. The technique complements our visualization suite by adding a spatial aggregation perspective: while Vis4 shows individual stellar positions with multi-attribute encoding (temperature, brightness, position), Vis5 reveals the overall density architecture of stellar distribution, providing insight into galactic structure visible in our local stellar neighborhood.

Interaction Design

1. Interactive Tooltips: Multiple visualizations implement hover-activated tooltips that reveal detailed star information without cluttering the display. In Visualization 2 (scatter plot), hovering displays the star's name, B-V color index, and visual magnitude. In Visualization 4 (sky map), tooltips show comprehensive data including celestial coordinates (RA/Dec), magnitude, B-V color, and spectral type. This follows the information visualization principle of "overview first, details on demand"—users can explore the overall pattern initially, then access specific information for individual stars as needed. The tooltips appear near the cursor and update dynamically as users move across the visualization, maintaining contextual awareness while providing precise measurements.

2. Highlight and Focus: Visualization 4 implements sophisticated focus+context interaction where hovering over a star enlarges it to 1.8× its normal size, increases opacity to full brightness, and adds a golden stroke, while simultaneously keeping surrounding stars visible for spatial context. This technique enables users to identify and examine individual stars even in the densest regions of the sky map where hundreds of points overlap. The enlargement is proportional and smooth, providing clear visual feedback about which element is currently selected without disorienting the user or losing the overall spatial structure.

3. Interactive Filtering (Sky Map - Visualization 4): The celestial sky map features comprehensive filtering capabilities that address the challenge of identifying trends in dense stellar distributions. Users can click filter options in the right-side legend to isolate stellar subpopulations by Temperature (Hot: B-V < 0.5, Medium: 0.5-1.2, Cool: B-V > 1.2) or Brightness (Bright: magnitude < 2, Medium: 2-4, Dim: > 4). When a filter is activated, stars matching the criterion remain at 80% opacity while non-matching stars dim to 10%, creating clear visual separation without completely removing context. The selected filter option in the legend highlights to indicate active state, providing immediate feedback. This filtering enables systematic exploration of spatial patterns—users can ask questions like "Where do hot blue stars cluster?" or "How do the brightest stars distribute across the sky?" and immediately see the answer through the filtered view. Multiple filters can be applied sequentially, and clicking "All" resets to the unfiltered state. This interaction directly supports analytical tasks by making subpopulation patterns visible that would be impossible to discern in the dense unfiltered display of 3,100 overlapping points.

4. Legend-Based Category Selection (Visualization 1): The spectral type bar chart includes an interactive legend where clicking on spectral classes (O, B, A, F, G, K, M) highlights or isolates those categories in the visualization. This enables comparative analysis—users can focus on specific stellar types to understand their frequency relative to others, or click multiple types to compare subgroups. The legend items provide visual feedback on hover and selection state, making the interaction mechanism clear and intuitive even for users unfamiliar with the dataset.

5. Trend Line Visualization: Visualization 4 (sky map) incorporates a cyan dashed linear regression trend line showing the overall distribution pattern of stars across Right Ascension and Declination. This statistical overlay helps users identify whether stars cluster along certain celestial coordinates—potentially indicating the galactic plane—or show more uniform distribution. The trend line remains visible during filtering operations, providing a consistent reference frame for comparing filtered subpopulations against the overall pattern. The dashed style distinguishes it from data elements while maintaining visibility.

6. Non-Interactive Density Aggregation (Visualization 5): The stellar density contour map intentionally lacks interactive controls, instead presenting an immediate, comprehensive view of concentration patterns across the celestial sphere. This design choice ensures all users instantly perceive the density structure without requiring active exploration. The smooth contour lines and color gradient (pale yellow to deep red) provide pre-attentive visual processing—users can identify high-density regions within milliseconds of viewing. This complements the interactive Visualization 4 by offering a different analytical mode: Vis4 enables detailed individual exploration, while Vis5 provides instant big-picture density understanding. The static presentation also ensures consistent interpretation across all viewers, making it ideal for presentations and reports where interaction isn't possible.

Summary and Future Work

Our visualizations reveal that cooler spectral types (F, G, K) dominate the catalog, with intermediate temperatures (B-V ≈ 0.62) and brightness (magnitude ≈ 5.06) being most common. The celestial sky map with interactive filtering demonstrates non-uniform spatial distribution, with different stellar types clustering in different sky regions. The density contour map (Vis5) reveals distinct high-concentration zones at specific Right Ascension ranges (particularly RA 60-90° and 270-300°), likely corresponding to our view through the Milky Way's galactic plane and spiral arm structures. These density patterns show that stellar distribution is highly non-uniform across the celestial sphere, reflecting the three-dimensional structure of our galaxy as projected onto the two-dimensional sky. Together, our five complementary visualizations provide comprehensive analysis from categorical (Vis1), relational (Vis2), distributional (Vis3), individual spatial (Vis4), and aggregated density (Vis5) perspectives.

Future work could include 3D spatial plots incorporating proper motion vectors, temporal animations of stellar motion, integration with Gaia DR3 for precise parallaxes and radial velocities, correction for observational selection effects to estimate true local stellar population demographics, and constellation mapping to connect abstract coordinates with familiar sky patterns.

Methodological Insights: This project demonstrates the importance of using multiple visualization techniques to fully understand complex datasets. Each visualization type—bar charts for categorical comparison, scatter plots for relationship analysis, density plots for frequency patterns, point maps for individual spatial analysis, and contour maps for aggregated density—reveals different aspects that would be invisible in any single view. The interactive filtering in Visualization 4 proved essential for identifying subpopulation patterns in dense data, directly addressing the professor's feedback about making trends visible. The combination of interactive exploration (Vis1, Vis2, Vis4) and static presentation (Vis3, Vis5) serves different analytical needs—interaction enables hypothesis testing and detailed investigation, while static views ensure all users immediately perceive key patterns. The careful choice of visual encodings—position for spatial accuracy, color for categorical differentiation and temperature gradients, size for magnitude, and contour intensity for density—demonstrates alignment between data attributes, perceptual effectiveness, and analytical tasks. This multi-faceted approach transforms raw astronomical measurements into accessible, interpretable insights about the structure and characteristics of bright stars in our galactic neighborhood.
